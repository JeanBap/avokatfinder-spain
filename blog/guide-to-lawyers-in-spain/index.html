Complete Guide to Finding a Lawyer in Spain

12 min read  ·  Spain Legal Guide  ·  Updated April 2026

TL;DR — Quick Summary

Finding an English-speaking lawyer in Spain starts with the national bar association (Consejo General de la Abogacía Española). Expect to pay EUR 150–350/hr for a qualified advocate. Always verify registration before engaging anyone. Most expat matters — property, immigration, employment, family — require a locally qualified lawyer.

Spain's Legal System

Spain operates under a civil law tradition. This means the law is codified in comprehensive written statutes — covering civil, commercial, criminal, and administrative matters — that judges interpret and apply. Unlike common law systems (UK, Ireland, USA), judicial precedent plays a secondary role; the written codes are the primary source of law.

Court proceedings in Spain are conducted in Spanish. All documents filed with the court must be in Spanish or accompanied by a certified translation. This linguistic requirement makes local legal representation practically essential for any expat involved in formal proceedings.

The court hierarchy runs from first-instance trial courts — with specialist divisions for commercial, labour, family, and administrative matters — through appellate courts, to a supreme court that handles legal principle questions only. Constitutional matters are handled by a separate constitutional court or tribunal.

Types of Legal Professionals in Spain

Not every person who calls themselves a "legal adviser" in Spain is a fully qualified lawyer. Understanding the distinctions protects you from poor advice and potential fraud.

The Qualified Advocate / Attorney

The core legal professional you need for most matters. In Spain, the title of qualified lawyer is legally protected. To practise, a lawyer must hold a qualifying law degree, complete a mandatory traineeship, pass bar examinations, and maintain active registration with the bar. The national regulatory body is the Consejo General de la Abogacía Española. You can verify any lawyer's current registration directly on their website — always do this before instructing anyone.

The Notario

A notary in Spain is a state-appointed legal official whose role is to authenticate, certify, and give legal force to important documents. Property transactions, company formations, and inheritance matters typically require notarial involvement. Crucially, the notary acts as an impartial public official — not as your advocate. If you are buying property or forming a company, you will need both a notary and your own lawyer.

Legal Consultants and Foreign-Qualified Lawyers

Some practitioners are qualified in another jurisdiction and advise exclusively on the law of that country. They cannot appear before Spain courts or advise on Spain domestic law. They are useful for cross-border matters but not a substitute for a locally qualified lawyer.

When You Need a Lawyer in Spain

Many expats delay seeking legal advice until a problem has escalated. Early advice almost always produces better outcomes at lower total cost. Engage a lawyer in Spain for:

  • Property purchase or sale — without exception; the risks of proceeding without legal representation in Spain are significant
  • Employment contract review or any workplace dispute, including dismissal
  • Immigration applications — visa applications, residence permits, renewals, and appeals
  • Business formation — company incorporation, shareholder agreements, commercial contracts
  • Family law — divorce, child arrangements, prenuptial agreements, and cross-border inheritance
  • Criminal matters — any investigation, interview under caution, or charge
  • Significant civil disputes — contract claims, landlord/tenant disputes, personal injury
  • Regulatory matters — dealings with tax authorities, licensing bodies, or government agencies

Consider a lawyer for: lease reviews before signing; letter of demand response; estate planning; and any matter involving a sum you cannot afford to lose.

How to Find a Qualified English-Speaking Lawyer in Spain

The most reliable starting point is the Consejo General de la Abogacía Española, which publishes a searchable register of all licensed lawyers in Spain. Many entries indicate language skills. Supplement this with:

  • The AvökatFinder directory — all listed firms have been verified for English-language capability and current bar registration
  • Your country's embassy in Spain — most maintain a list of English-speaking lawyers for nationals in difficulty
  • International Bar Association (IBA) referrals for complex cross-border matters
  • Specialist expat forums and community groups in cities like Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia

When shortlisting lawyers, ask specifically: Do you handle matters for English-speaking expats regularly? Do you have experience with [your specific issue]? Can you communicate primarily in English? What are your fees?

The First Meeting — What to Expect

Initial consultations in Spain typically cost EUR 120–220 for a 45–60 minute session. Some lawyers offer a free 15-minute introductory call. Use the first meeting to:

  • Explain your situation clearly and bring all relevant documents
  • Ask about the lawyer's specific experience with your type of matter
  • Understand the likely process, timeline, and cost range
  • Clarify how communication will work (email, phone, language)
  • Ask for a written fee agreement before any formal engagement

You are under no obligation to instruct after a consultation. It is entirely reasonable to meet two or three lawyers before deciding who to work with.

Verifying a Lawyer's Credentials

Before instructing any lawyer in Spain, confirm:

  1. Active bar registration via Consejo General de la Abogacía Española — a suspended or struck-off lawyer cannot legitimately represent you
  2. Professional indemnity insurance — ask directly; all practising lawyers in Spain are required to hold cover
  3. No disciplinary history — the bar association can provide this on request
  4. A written engagement letter setting out scope, fees, and conflict-of-interest confirmation
