Understanding Spain's Legal System

Spain uses a civil law (Código Civil + regional systems). In practical terms, this means legal rights and obligations are primarily defined by written statutes and codes rather than by judicial precedent. Judges apply the law as written, though case law does develop interpretive guidance over time.

For individuals and businesses from common-law countries (the UK, Ireland, USA, Australia), the key differences to note are:

  • Written contracts are paramount. Courts interpret contracts strictly according to their text, with less room for implied terms than in common law.
  • Notaries have formal legal authority. Many transactions — property sales, company incorporations, estate settlements — require notarial authentication. This is mandatory, not optional.
  • Inquisitorial court procedure. In civil litigation, judges take a more active role in investigating facts than in common-law adversarial systems.
  • Codified family law. Divorce, custody and inheritance rules are set by statutory codes; there is less judicial discretion than in common-law systems.

Working with a Spain-qualified lawyer who understands both the local system and, where relevant, EU law is essential for cross-border matters.

Lawyers vs Notaries in Spain: What Is the Difference?

What Does a Abogado Do?

A licensed abogado in Spain provides legal advice, drafts legal documents, represents clients in court, negotiates on behalf of clients, and handles disputes. They can advise on virtually any area of law and are bound by professional secrecy obligations to their clients. All abogados must be registered with the General Council of Spanish Lawyers (Consejo General de la Abogacía Española) and hold a law degree plus professional qualification.

What Does a Notario (known locally as notario) Do?

A notario in Spain is a state-appointed legal official who authenticates and certifies documents. Their role is distinct from a abogado: while a abogado advocates for a client, a notario is an impartial public officer. You will need a notario for:

  • Buying or selling real property (land or buildings)
  • Incorporating a company or changing its articles
  • Certifying powers of attorney
  • Certifying wills and estate settlements
  • Legalising and apostilling documents for use abroad

Notarial fees in Spain are typically regulated by a statutory fee scale and are calculated as a percentage of the transaction value, not an hourly rate.

What About Legal Aid?

Spain provides state-funded legal aid for individuals who cannot afford a lawyer, subject to income thresholds and the nature of the case. Criminal defence, asylum and some civil matters (particularly family law) are commonly covered. Contact the local bar association or court for eligibility criteria.

Legal Fees in Spain: What to Expect

Legal fees in Spain are not uniformly regulated (except for notarial fees and, in some areas, criminal legal aid). Rates vary significantly by city, specialisation and firm size.

Typical Hourly Rates

As a general guide, expect to pay €100–€500 per hour for a qualified abogado in Spain. This range reflects the difference between a sole practitioner in a regional city and a partner at a top-tier firm in Madrid.

  • Large international firms in Madrid: Higher end of the range or above
  • Mid-tier domestic firms: Mid-range
  • Regional practitioners: Lower end of the range
  • Sole practitioners / local offices: Often most affordable

Fixed-Fee Services

Many straightforward matters are handled on a fixed-fee basis in Spain, including:

  • Company incorporation
  • Simple will drafting
  • Residential lease review
  • Trademark registration
  • Debt recovery for undisputed debts

Contingency Fees

Contingency arrangements (where the lawyer takes a percentage of any damages recovered, and charges nothing if the case is lost) are permitted in some areas of law in Spain, though the rules vary. Ask your lawyer explicitly whether this arrangement is available and what percentage they would charge.

Notarial Fees

Notarial fees in Spain are typically calculated on a sliding scale based on the value of the transaction. For a property purchase, expect to pay between 0.5% and 1.5% of the purchase price in notarial fees, though the exact amount depends on the statutory schedule in force.

Currency and Payment

Legal fees in Spain are payable in EUR (Euro). Most firms accept bank transfer; some also accept card payment. Retainer payments (advance deposits held in a client account) are standard practice for ongoing matters.

Notaries (Notario) in Spain: A Detailed Guide

Notaries (known locally as notario) play a central role in Spain's legal system. Unlike in common-law countries, where notarisation is a relatively minor authentication service, Spain's notarios are senior legal professionals with state authority over specific classes of legal transaction.

When Do You Need a Notary in Spain?

You will need a notario for the following types of transaction in Spain:

  • Real estate: Sale and purchase deeds, mortgages, and easements must be notarially authenticated to be legally valid. Without notarial authentication, a property transfer cannot be registered.
  • Company law: Incorporation of most company types, amendments to articles of association, and shareholder resolutions often require notarial form.
  • Family and succession: Marriage contracts, adoption proceedings, wills and probate are commonly notarised in Spain.
  • Powers of attorney: Powers of attorney for property transactions or legal proceedings require notarial authentication to be recognised by courts and registries.

How to Find a Notary in Spain

Notaries (notarios) in Spain are typically listed in a separate register maintained by the notarial chamber or the Ministry of Justice. AvokatFinder includes notary offices as a separate category — search with the "notary" filter to find notarios in your city.

How Much Does a Notary Charge in Spain?

Notarial fees in Spain are generally regulated by a statutory fee schedule, unlike lawyer fees which are freely negotiated. Fees are usually calculated as a percentage of the transaction value. For guidance, request a fee estimate from the notary before the appointment; they are required to provide this on request.

How to Verify a Lawyer's Credentials in Spain

Protecting yourself from unqualified practitioners is straightforward if you follow these steps:

  1. Request bar registration number. Any qualified abogado in Spain should be able to provide their registration number immediately.
  2. Cross-check with the bar register. Visit the General Council of Spanish Lawyers (Consejo General de la Abogacía Española) and search for the lawyer's name and registration number.
  3. Check for disciplinary history. Most bar registers note whether a member has faced disciplinary action. A suspension or strike-off is a serious red flag.
  4. Confirm professional indemnity insurance. Licensed lawyers in Spain are required to maintain professional indemnity insurance. Ask for confirmation before engaging.
  5. Avoid cash-only arrangements. All fees should be payable to the law firm's client account, not personally to the lawyer in cash.

How the Bar Association Regulates Lawyers in Spain

Every licensed abogado in Spain is subject to oversight by the General Council of Spanish Lawyers (Consejo General de la Abogacía Española). Understanding how regulation works helps you know what protections you have as a client.

Admission to the Bar

To practise as a abogado in Spain, a lawyer must hold a recognised law degree, complete a period of supervised practice (typically one to three years as a trainee), pass a professional qualification examination, and be admitted to the bar. Foreign lawyers who trained outside Spain must pass an aptitude test or fulfil re-qualification requirements before they can practise independently.

Continuing Professional Development

Registered abogados in Spain are required to complete a minimum number of continuing professional development (CPD) hours each year to maintain their bar registration. This ensures that practising lawyers keep up to date with legislative changes, new case law and evolving professional standards.

Professional Indemnity Insurance

All licensed abogados in Spain must maintain professional indemnity insurance to cover claims arising from negligent advice or errors in legal work. This insurance protects clients who suffer financial loss due to a lawyer's mistake. Always ask your lawyer to confirm their insurance status before instructing them.

Complaints and Disciplinary Procedures

If you have a complaint about a abogado in Spain, you should first attempt to resolve it directly with the lawyer or their firm. If that fails, you can submit a formal complaint to the General Council of Spanish Lawyers (Consejo General de la Abogacía Española). Serious misconduct can result in suspension or permanent disbarment. The bar association maintains a public record of disciplinary sanctions.

Client Funds and Accounts

Lawyers who handle client money in Spain must maintain separate client accounts, segregated from the firm's own funds. This is a regulatory requirement. If a lawyer asks you to transfer money to a personal account rather than a firm client account, treat this as a serious warning sign.

Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) in Spain

Not every legal dispute needs to go to court. Spain has a developed framework for alternative dispute resolution (ADR) that can save significant time and cost.

Mediation

Mediation involves a neutral third party helping disputing parties reach a voluntary settlement. It is widely available in Spain for commercial disputes, family law matters (particularly divorce and custody) and neighbour disputes. Mediated agreements are enforceable once formalised in a written settlement agreement, which a abogado can draft and review.

Arbitration

Commercial arbitration is an established dispute-resolution mechanism in Spain, particularly for cross-border contracts. Arbitration clauses are commonly included in commercial agreements, and awards are enforceable internationally under the 1958 New York Convention. Spain has arbitration institutions that can administer both domestic and international proceedings.

Online Dispute Resolution

For consumer disputes under €5,000 within the EU, the European Commission's Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) platform is available. Consumers in Spain can use this platform to resolve cross-border e-commerce disputes without needing a abogado, although legal advice is recommended for complex matters.

Should You Go to Court?

Courts in Spain can be slow and expensive. Before committing to litigation, ask your abogado whether mediation or arbitration is a realistic alternative. In many cases, a well-structured ADR process achieves a faster and cheaper resolution than going to trial — and preserves the business relationship where that matters.

Foreign nationals wishing to establish a company in Spain need legal advice on the most appropriate corporate structure, the registration process, regulatory requirements and, where applicable, sector-specific licencing. The notario plays a role in company formation: most company types in Spain require notarial authentication of the incorporation documents.

Employment and Labour Rights

Expatriate employees in Spain should be aware of their rights under local employment law, which applies regardless of the nationality of the employer or employee. Key areas include minimum notice periods, redundancy entitlements, non-compete clauses, and the enforceability of employment contracts governed by foreign law.

Inheritance and Estate Planning

EU Regulation 650/2012 (the European Succession Regulation) allows EU citizens to elect that their estate be governed by the law of their country of nationality rather than the country where they reside. This has significant implications for expatriates in Spain. Cross-border estate planning requires specialised legal advice from a lawyer with expertise in both Spain's succession law and the law of your home country.

Ten Practical Tips for Hiring a Lawyer in Spain

  1. Check bar registration first. Verify credentials via the General Council of Spanish Lawyers (Consejo General de la Abogacía Española) before paying any fees.
  2. Get a written fee agreement. Never start work without a signed engagement letter specifying fees, scope, and disbursements.
  3. Clarify who will handle your file. At large firms, a partner may pitch and a junior associate may do the work. Know who you are actually hiring.
  4. Ask about timelines. Get a realistic estimate of how long your matter will take — and insist on regular updates.
  5. Understand the billing cycle. Monthly invoices with time-recording breakdowns are standard. Review each invoice promptly and query any entries you do not recognise.
  6. Keep copies of everything. Store all correspondence, contracts, and legal opinions securely. Your lawyer's file belongs to them; your copies belong to you.
  7. Ask about alternatives to court. Mediation and arbitration are often faster and cheaper. Ask your abogado whether ADR is appropriate for your matter.
  8. Language matters. If you do not speak Spain's language, engage an English-speaking abogado from the outset. Translation of documents is an additional cost; budget for it.
  9. Second opinions are fine. For significant matters, getting a second legal opinion from a different firm is entirely normal and often valuable.
  10. Understand conflict checks. A reputable firm will check for conflicts of interest before taking your case. If they do not, ask them to confirm they have done so.

Glossary of Legal Terms You Will Encounter in Spain

When working with a abogado in Spain, you may encounter the following legal terms. Understanding them will help you communicate more effectively and follow the progress of your matter.

Power of Attorney
A legal document authorising one person (the attorney-in-fact) to act on behalf of another (the principal) in legal or financial matters. In Spain, powers of attorney for property transactions must be authenticated by a notario.
Affidavit
A written statement confirmed under oath or by solemn affirmation. Affidavits are used in court proceedings and administrative processes as evidence of facts.
Due Diligence
The investigation of a business, property or individual before entering a contract or completing a transaction. Legal due diligence reviews contracts, litigation history, regulatory compliance and title to assets.
Injunction
A court order requiring a party to do — or to stop doing — a specific act. Interim injunctions can be obtained urgently to prevent ongoing harm while the main case is resolved.
Statute of Limitations
The maximum period after an event within which legal proceedings may be brought. In Spain, limitation periods vary by type of claim. Missing the limitation period generally bars your claim entirely.
Notarisation / Apostille
Notarisation is the authentication of a document by a notario. An apostille is a certification added to a notarised document to make it recognised in countries party to the Hague Convention — useful for documents that need to be used internationally.
Lien
A legal claim over a property as security for a debt or obligation. In Spain, mortgage liens are registered in the land registry and must be discharged before a property can be sold with clean title.
Retainer
An advance payment made to a law firm, held in a client account and drawn down as work is completed and invoiced. Retainers are common practice for ongoing legal relationships in Spain.
