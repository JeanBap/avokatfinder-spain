Nota Simple (Land Registry Extract)

An official summary extract from the Spanish Land Registry (Registro de la Propiedad) showing a property's ownership, charges, mortgages, and encumbrances.

Home › Legal Glossary › Nota Simple (Land Registry Extract)
TL;DR An official summary extract from the Spanish Land Registry (Registro de la Propiedad) showing a property's ownership, charges, mortgages, and encumbrances.

The nota simple is a short informational extract issued by the Registro de la Propiedad (Spanish Land Registry) that summarises the key details of a specific property: its registered owner, cadastral reference, physical description, and any charges, mortgages, easements, or legal proceedings attached to it. It is the essential due diligence document for any property purchase in Spain.

A nota simple is not a certificate of ownership — it is an informational document, not a certified copy of the registry entry. However, it is widely accepted by notaries, banks, estate agents, and lawyers as evidence of the property's current registered state. Most Spanish notaries will not proceed with a purchase deed without having reviewed a recent nota simple.

You can obtain a nota simple online through the Colegio de Registradores de España portal (registradores.org) by entering the property's full address or registry coordinates. The fee is approximately €9. The document is typically issued within one to two business days. A paper version is available at any Land Registry office for a slightly higher fee.

The nota simple shows three key sections: the description section (finca registral — parcel number and property details), the ownership section (titularidad — registered owner and acquisition date), and the charges section (cargas — mortgages, usufructs, easements, tax liens, and court embargo notices). A clean nota simple with no cargas is a positive signal but must still be cross-checked with the Catastro.

Request a fresh nota simple dated within 24–48 hours of signing any contract. Registry entries can change rapidly — a mortgage can be registered, or an embargo placed, between the time of an earlier check and the day of signing. Your Spanish property lawyer should obtain the final nota simple the morning of the notarial deed.

⚠ Common Mistake: Relying on a nota simple obtained by the seller or estate agent weeks earlier. Always obtain a fresh nota simple the day before signing — embargoes and charges can appear overnight.
💡 Expert Tip: Cross-reference the nota simple with the Catastro reference (referencia catastral) to ensure the physical and legal descriptions match. Discrepancies — such as a difference in surface area — can cause complications at notary.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a nota simple the same as the title deed (escritura)?

No. The escritura pública is the notarially authenticated deed of purchase that is registered in the Land Registry. The nota simple is a summary extract of what is currently registered — it describes the current state of the title without reproducing the full deed. Both are important but serve different purposes.

What is a carga in the nota simple?

A carga is any encumbrance registered against the property — mortgages (hipotecas), usufructs (usufructos), easements (servidumbres), fiscal embargoes (embargos fiscales), or court attachment orders (anotaciones de embargo). Cargas that are not discharged at completion transfer with the property to the new owner.

Can I search the Land Registry by owner name?

No. Spanish Land Registry searches require the property's registry coordinates (municipio, finca number) or the full address. The system is property-based, not person-based. This means a seller's other liabilities elsewhere do not appear on the nota simple for the specific property you are buying.
