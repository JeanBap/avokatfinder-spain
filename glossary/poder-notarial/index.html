What is Poder Notarial (Power of Attorney Spain)?

A Spanish notarised power of attorney — authorising a named representative to act on your behalf for property transactions, NIE applications, company acts, or other legal matters.

Key Facts — Poder Notarial (Power of Attorney Spain)
  • Executed before Spanish notario (in Spain) or foreign notary + apostille (abroad)
  • Foreign powers: require apostille + sworn Spanish translation (traductor jurado)
  • Poder especial (specific) preferred over poder general for property transactions
  • No statutory expiry — but notaries/registries may require powers issued within 3–6 months
  • Automatically revoked on death or legal incapacity of the principal

A poder notarial — power of attorney — is a legal document executed before a Spanish notario (or, for foreign-based principals, before a foreign notary with apostille) that authorises a named person (the apoderado or representative) to act on behalf of the grantor (the poderdante) for specific purposes. In Spanish legal practice, the poder notarial is routinely used by non-resident buyers, sellers, and company representatives who cannot be physically present in Spain for a notarial act.

For Spanish property transactions, the poder notarial is particularly useful. Non-resident buyers frequently grant a poder to their Spanish property lawyer, enabling the lawyer to sign the preliminary contract (CPCV or arras), apply for the NIE, open a Spanish bank account, sign the final escritura, and register the property — all without the buyer needing to travel to Spain for each step. This is the most common way international buyers complete Spanish property purchases remotely.

A poder notarial granted outside Spain must comply with the Apostille Convention (Convenio de la Haya) for it to be valid in Spain. This means: the document is executed before a notary in the country where you are based; the foreign notary's signature is verified by the relevant national authority (the apostille); the entire document is then officially translated into Spanish by a sworn translator (traductor jurado). Without the apostille and sworn translation, a foreign-executed poder will not be accepted by a Spanish notario or the Land Registry.

Powers of attorney in Spain can be general (poder general) — covering a wide range of acts — or specific (poder especial) — covering a defined, limited set of acts such as signing a specific property purchase deed. For property transactions, Spanish notaries generally prefer a poder especial that is precisely worded to cover the specific acts required, as this provides greater legal certainty and reduces the risk of the document being rejected. The poder should name the property by its reference data, specify the price range and key terms, and name the specific parties where known.

Poders notariales have no fixed expiry in Spain unless a validity period is stated in the document itself or unless the principal dies or becomes incapacitated (which automatically revokes the poder). However, Spanish notaries and registries are increasingly requiring powers to be issued within a relatively recent period (often 3–6 months) for sensitive transactions, to guard against the possibility that circumstances have changed. If your poder was issued more than 6 months ago, check with your lawyer whether a fresh one is needed before proceeding.

Common Mistake: Obtaining a poder notarial abroad without checking whether an apostille is required for Spain. If your home country is a signatory to the 1961 Hague Convention (most countries are), an apostille is sufficient. If not, full legalisation through diplomatic channels is needed. Also ensure the sworn translation is made by a Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs-accredited traductor jurado — not just any bilingual translator.
Expert Tip: When instructing your Spanish lawyer to act under a poder notarial, include a specific clause permitting them to sign any ancillary documents required by the notario on the day of completion — purchase deeds sometimes include additional declarations or consents that were not anticipated when the poder was drafted. A broadly but specifically worded poder prevents last-minute complications.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I apply for a Spanish NIE with a power of attorney?

Yes. This is the standard arrangement for non-resident property buyers. You grant your Spanish lawyer a poder notarial, and they apply for your NIE at the Oficina de Extranjería or Comisaría de Policía on your behalf. You do not need to be present in Spain for the NIE application itself.

How long does it take to get an apostilled power of attorney?

The timeline depends on your home country. In the UK, an apostille can be obtained from the Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office (FCDO) in 1–5 working days for postal applications. In the US, apostilles are issued at state level — timelines vary by state but are typically 3–10 business days. Once apostilled, the sworn Spanish translation adds 2–5 days.

Can I revoke a Spanish power of attorney?

Yes. A poder notarial can be revoked at any time by the principal (poderdante) by executing a revocation (revocación de poder) before a Spanish notario. The revocation should be notified to the apoderado and, where the poder was used for registered acts, a note of revocation registered at the relevant registry. For urgent revocations, seek immediate legal advice.
