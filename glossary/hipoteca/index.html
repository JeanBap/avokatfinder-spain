What is Hipoteca (Spanish Mortgage)?

A Spanish mortgage (hipoteca) — the legal instrument securing a loan against real estate, governed by the 2019 Mortgage Law which significantly strengthened borrower protections.

Key Facts — Hipoteca (Spanish Mortgage)
  • Ley 5/2019: bank pays AJD on new mortgages; 10-day pre-signing cooling off period
  • Non-residents: typically 60–70% LTV (lower than the 80% for residents)
  • Tasación (appraisal) required — costs €300–€600, paid by borrower
  • Variable-rate mortgages typically linked to Euribor
  • TAE = true annual cost (use this to compare offers, not the nominal rate)

A hipoteca in Spain is the legal instrument by which a lender (typically a bank) takes security over real estate as collateral for a loan. When you take out a Spanish mortgage, you grant the bank a hipoteca over the property; this is registered at the Registro de la Propiedad (Land Registry) as a charge on the title. The hipoteca remains on the register until the loan is fully repaid and formally cancelled (cancelación registral de la hipoteca).

Spanish mortgage law was significantly reformed by the Ley 5/2019, known as the Ley Reguladora de los Contratos de Crédito Inmobiliario or Ley Hipotecaria. Key changes introduced by this law include: the bank (not the borrower) paying the AJD stamp duty on new mortgages; mandatory pre-contractual information and a minimum 10-day reflection period before signing; compulsory free independent legal advice from a notary before signing; stricter limits on floor clauses (cláusulas suelo) and early repayment penalties; and clearer rules on variable-rate products. This law substantially increased consumer protection for Spanish mortgage borrowers.

Spanish mortgages for non-residents (non-Spanish tax residents) are available but typically come with lower loan-to-value ratios than those offered to residents. Most Spanish banks lend non-residents a maximum of 60–70% of the property's appraisal value (tasación), compared to up to 80% for residents. Non-residents should also expect slightly higher interest rates. Typical documentation required includes: recent tax returns from your home country, payslips or proof of self-employment income, existing property mortgage statements, recent bank statements, and a copy of your passport and NIE.

The tasación — official property appraisal — is a mandatory step in obtaining a Spanish mortgage. The bank will require an appraisal by an approved tasadora (appraisal company). You typically pay for the tasación (€300–€600 depending on the property), and the bank uses the lower of the tasación value and the purchase price as the basis for calculating the maximum loan amount. The tasación can also be used by you as an independent benchmark of the property's value.

When comparing Spanish mortgage products, pay attention to the TAE (Tasa Anual Equivalente) — the equivalent of APR — rather than just the nominal interest rate, as the TAE incorporates all associated costs and gives a true like-for-like comparison. Spanish mortgages may be linked to the Euribor (the most common variable rate benchmark in Spain), to a fixed rate, or to a mixed rate. Since 2022–2023, rising Euribor rates have made fixed-rate mortgages increasingly popular among buyers seeking payment certainty.

Common Mistake: Non-resident buyers often approach Spanish banks without having prepared the required documentation bundle in advance, causing significant delays. Compile your last two years' tax returns (apostilled if applicable), recent payslips or proof of income, and a summary of existing liabilities before starting mortgage conversations — this is what every Spanish bank will ask for.
Expert Tip: Under the 2019 Ley Hipotecaria, you have the right to free independent legal advice at the notary before signing your mortgage deed. Use this appointment to ask the notary to clarify any clauses you do not understand — the notary's obligation is to ensure you genuinely understand what you are signing. Do not waive this meeting.
Related terms: NIE ESCRITURA NOTARIO

Frequently Asked Questions

Can non-residents get a mortgage in Spain?

Yes. Most major Spanish banks (Santander, BBVA, CaixaBank, Sabadell, Bankinter) offer mortgage products to non-resident foreign nationals. Typical LTV is 60–70% for non-residents versus up to 80% for residents. You will need to demonstrate income from your home country, provide apostilled financial documents, and have a Spanish NIE number.

What does a Spanish mortgage cost beyond the interest rate?

Beyond interest, a Spanish mortgage involves: the tasación (appraisal fee, €300–€600 paid by borrower), life insurance (bancassurance, often required by the bank), home insurance (compulsory), and any opening commission (comisión de apertura, increasingly rare). The 2019 law transferred the AJD stamp duty cost to the bank. The TAE (APR) figure incorporates all costs — use it for comparison.

What is IRPH and should I avoid it?

IRPH (Índice de Referencia de Préstamos Hipotecarios) is an alternative Spanish mortgage reference rate historically higher than Euribor. Many mortgages written before 2013 used IRPH. The Court of Justice of the EU has ruled on IRPH transparency, and Spanish courts have been addressing historical IRPH claims. If your existing Spanish mortgage is linked to IRPH, consult a Spanish mortgage litigation lawyer.
