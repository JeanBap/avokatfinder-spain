Contrato de Arrendamiento (Rental Contract)

A Spanish rental contract — the agreement between landlord and tenant governing the lease of residential or commercial property under Spanish tenancy law.

TL;DR A Spanish rental contract — the agreement between landlord and tenant governing the lease of residential or commercial property under Spanish tenancy law.

The contrato de arrendamiento (rental contract) is the binding agreement between a landlord (arrendador) and a tenant (arrendatario) for the use of a property in exchange for rent. Residential rental contracts in Spain are governed primarily by the Ley de Arrendamientos Urbanos (LAU — Urban Tenancies Act), which sets minimum tenant protections that cannot be contracted out of. Commercial leases operate under a more flexible regime.

For residential leases under the LAU, tenants have the right to remain in the property for up to five years (seven years if the landlord is a company), regardless of any shorter term written in the contract. After the mandatory period, the contract renews annually unless either party gives notice. The landlord cannot terminate early except in limited circumstances (such as requiring the property for personal use).

Spanish residential tenancy law caps the security deposit (fianza) at one month's rent for residential leases. Landlords may additionally request a complementary guarantee (garantía adicional) of up to two months in non-professional landlord arrangements. The deposit must be lodged with the regional housing authority (organismo autonómico) — failure to do so is a regulatory offence.

Rent for residential properties is subject to annual increase caps tied to the IPC (índice de precios al consumo — consumer price index) or a lower reference index introduced by recent reforms. Landlords in areas designated as "tensa" (stressed rental markets) face stricter caps. Expats should always verify the current applicable cap when renewing.

Contracts must be registered with the regional tax authority (Agencia Tributaria Autonómica) by the landlord, who owes income tax on rental income. As a tenant, you are not responsible for the landlord's tax declarations, but you should ensure the contract is registered and that you receive proper receipts. An unregistered contract does not affect the tenant's rights but creates risks if disputes arise.

⚠ Common Mistake: Signing a short-term contract (e.g. 11 months) thinking you are not protected by LAU. Spanish courts have repeatedly held that sham short-term contracts to avoid LAU protections are void — the five-year minimum applies regardless.
💡 Expert Tip: Before signing, request a nota simple for the property to verify the landlord is the registered owner (or has authority to rent). Tenants who pay rent to non-owners have limited legal protection.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the landlord ask for six months rent in advance?

No. Spanish law limits deposits and guarantees to what is permitted by the LAU. Requests for large advance rent payments beyond the regulated deposit and additional guarantee are unlawful for residential tenancies. Paying such amounts leaves the tenant with limited recourse if disputes arise.

What notice must I give to terminate the contract?

Residential tenants must give 30 days written notice before the lease termination date. Failure to give proper notice may result in liability for an additional month's rent. Landlords seeking to end the tenancy have stricter requirements and limited grounds during the mandatory period.

What is a certificado de empadronamiento and why do landlords ask for it?

The empadronamiento is municipal registration at the property address. Landlords sometimes ask tenants to present one as evidence of residency. It is issued by the ayuntamiento (town hall) and is also required for many administrative processes in Spain — opening bank accounts, school enrolment, and accessing some public services.
